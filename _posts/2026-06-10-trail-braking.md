"Slow in, fast out" gets you started. **Trail braking** is what you graduate to — carrying a
trailing amount of front brake past the entry and releasing it as you add lean. Done right,
it makes a bike turn better and gives you margin when a corner tightens.

<!--more-->

## Why it works

Braking compresses the front suspension and steepens the steering geometry, so the bike
wants to turn. Trailing the brake keeps that geometry loaded exactly when you're asking the
bike to change direction.

## The technique

1. Brake hard and early while the bike is upright.
2. As you begin to lean, **gradually release** the front brake.
3. Trade brake pressure for lean angle — as one goes down, the other comes up.
4. Off the brake by the apex, then roll on the throttle.

Think of it as one continuous motion, not two separate events.

## Building it safely

Start at 40% on a road you know. Feel for the front tire loading up — the same feedback you
learned to read in [Reading Your Tires Like a Map](/notes/maintenance/reading-tires/). This
is the skill that makes [Angeles Crest](/notes/routes/angeles-crest/) flow, and it becomes
essential the moment you add [group riding](/notes/craft/group-riding/) and closing speeds.

> Smoothness first. Speed is a byproduct of smoothness, never the goal.
